private credit, publicly stressed

Private credit is basically loans made outside the traditional banking system. A company needs money, but the bank won't lend to them. Too risky, too messy, whatever. So private credit firms step in. They lend the money directly. No regulators breathing down their necks the way they do with banks. No public filings. No transparency. That's the thing right there.

For years, this worked fine for big institutional investors. Pension funds, endowments, insurance companies. They had the sophistication to evaluate risk. They had skin in the game. But over the past year or so, the firms pushing private credit started getting aggressive about retail. Regular people. 401(k)s. Your retirement account. And that's when my antenna went up.

The pitch was simple: you want higher returns? Stocks and bonds aren't cutting it anymore. Private credit offers yields that beat the market. Eight percent, nine percent, sometimes double digits. It sounds too good to be true because it probably is.

Here's what bothered me. The entire growth of this industry has felt propped up by artificial demand and marketing, not genuine economic fundamentals. Wall Street was pushing it hard because Wall Street wanted to move money around and collect fees. Two trillion dollars in the private credit market right now. That's enormous. And a huge chunk of that growth came from hype, not from actual borrowers getting healthier or credit getting safer.

Then you've got the leverage problem. These private credit firms don't just lend your money. They borrow money themselves to amplify returns. That's called back-leverage. It means when things go wrong, they go wrong fast. It's not just the loan that fails. It's the loan plus the borrowed money on top of it, all collapsing at once.

And the borrowers? A lot of them are software companies. Risky, overvalued, vulnerable to AI disruption. These aren't stable mid-market manufacturers. These are companies that could get disrupted overnight. The private credit world was lending to them like they were blue chips.

There's a historical parallel that keeps nagging at me. Michael Lewis wrote about this in The Big Short. The mortgage crisis wasn't an accident. It was architecture. Wall Street took subprime mortgages, loans to people who couldn't actually afford them, and packaged them into bonds with AAA ratings. The rating agencies signed off. Institutions bought them thinking they were buying safety. Retail investors followed. When it all collapsed, the ratings meant nothing.

What bothers me is that private credit has the same DNA. The structure is different, but the logic is identical. Take risky loans. Bundle them. Dress them up with promises of stable yield. Get institutions hooked first. Then open the door to retail. Market it as access. Market it as opportunity. By the time people realize the risk, the money's already committed. With mortgages, at least there was collateral, houses. With private credit to software companies facing disruption, what's the collateral? A company that could be obsolete in two years. But the marketing doesn't lead with that. It leads with the yield.

Last month, JPMorgan marked down the value of loans held by private credit funds. Blue Owl, Apollo, KKR, Ares, all of them took hits. Blue Owl actually froze redemptions. That means retail investors who wanted their money back couldn't get it. Blue Owl's fund sold roughly 34 percent of its portfolio and used the proceeds to repay a credit facility from Goldman Sachs. That's the thing about private credit. It promises liquidity, but when you need it, it evaporates.

The default numbers are real now. The default rate among US corporate borrowers of private credit rose to a record 9.2 percent in 2025, following a previous record of 8.1 percent in 2024. UBS laid out a worst-case scenario where defaults in the sector could climb to 15 percent. That's a catastrophe waiting to happen.

And here's what really gets me: institutional investors saw this coming. They started pulling money out months ago. But at the exact same time, Wall Street was marketing private credit hard to people with 401(k)s. People who don't have time to do deep credit analysis. People who trust the branding and the promises.

It feels like a classic bait and switch. The smart money exits. The retail money enters. And when the defaults spike, regular people hold the bag.

The part nobody's connecting: private equity

What concerns me even more is how little attention the private equity side of this is getting. Private credit and private equity are deeply intertwined, but nobody seems to be talking about the correlation. Private credit is lending to the same kinds of companies that private equity owns. When those companies struggle, it hits both sides of the ledger at once.

The private equity industry is sitting on roughly 31,000 unsold companies valued at about $3.7 trillion. That backlog is enormous. According to PwC's analysis, somewhere between 4,000 and 6,500 exits have been delayed over the past two years alone. More than 63 percent of active portfolio companies have been held for more than four years. Average holding periods for buyout deals hit 6.4 years in 2025. That used to be five to six years. The math is stretching.

Distributions to investors, the actual cash coming back, have been at drought levels for four consecutive years. Bain's data shows the distribution metric sitting at around 14 percent, a level not seen since the 2008-09 financial crisis. And arguably worse, because back then it lasted two years. This has lasted four. The head of Bain's global private equity practice said this is a "five-plus year problem" that isn't going away in 2025 or 2026.

Last year there were some blockbuster IPOs. CoreWeave went public in April 2025 and its shares climbed roughly 185 percent from the IPO price. The headlines were optimistic. But the recovery was incredibly top-heavy. Exit value jumped 41 percent, but the total number of exits actually declined 15 percent. 78 percent of all exit value was concentrated in mega exits. The mid-market, where most of these companies actually sit, remained frozen.

And this year the IPO pipeline is weakening. The softening of the AI trade, tariff uncertainty, all of it is making companies and investors hesitant. Several offerings got postponed or canceled in the second quarter. The window that was supposed to open wider is narrowing again.

EQT's CEO warned that as many as 80 percent of private equity firms could effectively become zombies over the next decade. Operational only to manage existing portfolios but unable to raise fresh capital because they can't return cash to investors. That's not a fringe prediction. That's the CEO of one of Europe's largest PE firms.

The ripple effects are what keep me up at night. When private equity firms can't exit companies, those companies don't get fresh investment. They cut costs. They lay off workers. In 2025 alone, private equity-related bankruptcies resulted in at least 36,800 layoffs from just 13 bankruptcies. That's a small sample. Scale it up against the 31,000 companies in that backlog, many of which were acquired at inflated prices during the easy-money years, and you start to see the shape of something much larger.

Meanwhile, the secondary market is exploding. It exceeded $200 billion for the first time in 2025. NAV loans have swelled to an estimated $150 billion. These are workarounds. They're not solutions. They're ways of pushing the reckoning further down the road.

I want to be clear about something. I am not familiar with the mechanics of private equity to the extent that I can foretell whether this will be as outsized of a fallout as 2008. I genuinely don't know. But the architecture feels similar. The risk is concentrated. The transparency is minimal. The retail exposure is growing. And the people who understand it best are the ones moving fastest to protect themselves.

I've been wary of private credit from the start. Not because I'm paranoid. But because the math doesn't work. You can't get sustainable eight percent yields in a four percent interest rate world without taking on massive risk. And that risk is being sold to people who don't understand it.

That's where I stand on this.
